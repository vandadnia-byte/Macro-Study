Topic 01 · Output

GDP & Economic Output

GDP is the total value of everything a country produces. It is the single most important number in macroeconomics — and it's just four things added together.

In plain terms. Add the price of every haircut, car, coffee and government service produced in a country for a year — that total is GDP.

Consumer spending is the largest part of GDP in most economies — typically 55–70%. When confidence falls, the whole economy feels it.

Four ways to measure the same thing
Expenditure
Add up all spending: C + I + G + NX. The most common method — what everyone paid.
Income
Sum all incomes earned: wages, profits, rents, interest. Should equal expenditure.
Production
Value added at each stage. Avoids double-counting intermediate goods like steel.
Real vs nominal
Real strips out inflation. 5% nominal growth with 3% inflation is only 2% real.
Per capita
GDP ÷ population. The fairer measure of average living standards.
PPP-adjusted
Adjusts for price differences. £1 buys far more in Delhi than in Geneva.
Topic 02 · Fluctuations

The Business Cycle

Economies don't grow in a straight line. They expand, peak, contract and trough — over and over, throughout history.

In plain terms. Think of the economy breathing — it expands (inhale) and contracts (exhale). The cycle repeats, and no expansion lasts forever.
Output over time — the cycle wave
Expansion — the good times
GDP rises, unemployment falls, businesses invest, consumers spend confidently. Like spring after winter — everything is blooming.
A road trip at 70mph on a clear motorway: music up, fuel full, the whole team confident you'll arrive.
Unemployment falls
Businesses need workers. Vacancies rise. The jobless find work within weeks.
Spending rises
People with jobs and confidence spend freely — homes, cars, holidays.
Investment booms
Firms build factories and launch products. Future capacity grows.
Asset prices rise
Stocks and property climb. Wealth effects encourage yet more spending.
Wages increase
Competition for workers pushes pay up, especially near full employment.
Credit expands
Banks lend freely. Low rates make borrowing cheap. Leverage builds.
Peak — maximum output
The economy runs at full capacity. Unemployment near its natural rate. Inflation picks up as everyone spends and wages climb.
You've reached the top of the hill. The view is great — but the only way forward is downhill.
Warning signs. Inflation above 3%, unemployment below NAIRU, stock valuations at record highs, banks lending aggressively, asset prices outpacing incomes.
Contraction (recession) — the hard times
GDP falls. Two negative quarters = official recession. Firms cut investment, lay off workers, and confidence collapses.
Winter has arrived. Everything slows — people stay home, spend less, and wait for spring.
GDP falls
Two consecutive quarters of negative growth = official recession.
Unemployment rises
Firms lay off workers, who then spend less — worsening the slump.
Confidence collapses
Uncertainty delays investment. Consumers save rather than spend.
Credit tightens
Banks turn cautious. Lending dries up, choking spending further.
Trough — rock bottom, and the turn
The lowest point. Output, employment and confidence at their worst — but this is where recovery begins. The dark before dawn.
After weeks of illness you feel terrible — but that's the turning point. Things can only improve from here.
At the trough. Central banks cut rates. Governments spend. Pent-up demand builds. The cycle turns and expansion begins again.

1991–2001 · The Dotcom Boom
Internet commercialisation plus the Cold War peace dividend. Unemployment fell 7.8% → 4%, markets tripled. Ended when the dotcom bubble burst — profitless companies collapsed overnight.

2003–2007 · The Credit Bubble
Easy credit, housing booms across the US, UK, Spain and Ireland, and China driving commodity demand. A classic Minsky moment — "stability is destabilising." Ended in the worst crisis since 1929.
10s
128 months · record
2009–2020 · The Long Recovery
The longest expansion ever — but also the slowest. The Fed held rates near zero for seven years and deployed $4 trillion in QE. Unemployment fell 10% → 3.5%. Ended abruptly by COVID-19.
20s
2 months
2020 · The COVID Crash
Shortest but sharpest recession on record. GDP fell 10% in a single quarter; US unemployment hit 14.7%. An extraordinary $10tn+ global response made the recovery surprisingly fast.

Topic 03 · Prices

Inflation & Prices

Inflation is the rate at which prices rise across the economy. A little is healthy. Too much is devastating.

In plain terms. If your weekly shop cost £100 last year and £102 now, that's 2% inflation — the sweet spot central banks aim for.
Inflation simulator
Money supply growth4%
Demand-pull pressure+2%
Supply shock0%
Wage-price spiral0%
Inflation near 2% — healthy. The target leaves room to cut rates in a recession without risking deflation.
Rate vs 2% target
Inflation rate2% target
The types of inflation
Demand-pull
Too much money chasing too few goods. Overheating — everyone spending. Late-expansion inflation.
Cost-push
Supply shocks raise costs (oil, wages). Passed to consumers. Hardest for central banks to fix.
Built-in spiral
Workers expect inflation, demand wage rises; firms raise prices. Self-fulfilling.
Monetary
Printing money raises M without raising output. Prices must rise (MV=PQ).
Deflation
Falling prices. Consumers delay buying — a trap, as in Japan's Lost Decade.
Hyperinflation
Over 50%/month. Currency trust collapses. Zimbabwe 2008 hit 89 sextillion %.
Topic 04 · Labour

Unemployment & Labour

Not all unemployment is the same. Knowing the types is the key to understanding what policy can actually fix.

In plain terms. Some unemployment is normal and even healthy (people between jobs). The dangerous kind is cyclical — caused by recessions — and that's what policy targets.
Phillips curve simulator
Economic stimulusNeutral
Labour flexibility5/10
Near NAIRU — the natural rate. Some unemployment always exists from people switching jobs. Policy can't eliminate it.
Inflation vs unemployment
Phillips curveCurrentNAIRU
The four types of unemployment
Frictional
Between jobs by choice — searching for something better. Normal and healthy.
Structural
Skills mismatch — jobs exist but workers can't do them. Needs retraining, not stimulus.
Cyclical
Caused by recessions. The main target of fiscal and monetary stimulus.
Seasonal
Tied to seasons — tourism, farming. Predictable, not a sign of weakness.
NAIRU
The floor below which unemployment can't fall without accelerating inflation (~4–5%).
Participation
% of working-age people in the workforce. Falls in recessions, masking true joblessness.
The long-run twist. Short-run, lower unemployment means higher inflation. But long-run the tradeoff vanishes — Friedman and Phelps showed the curve is vertical at NAIRU. You cannot permanently trade inflation for jobs.
Topic 05 · Central Banks

Monetary Policy

Central banks — the Fed, Bank of England, ECB — use interest rates and money supply to stabilise prices and support growth.

In plain terms. Inflation too high? Raise rates so borrowing costs more and people spend less. Economy weak? Cut rates so borrowing is cheap and spending picks up.
Run a central bank
Policy interest rate4.5%
Quantitative easingOff
Reserve requirements10%
Forward guidanceNeutral
Rate at 4.5% — moderately tight. Borrowing is somewhat expensive, cooling inflation while keeping growth positive.
GDPInflationUnemployment
The tools, explained
Interest rates
The primary lever. Raise to cool, cut to stimulate. Works with a 12–18 month lag.
Quantitative easing
Buying bonds to inject cash when rates hit zero. Pushes down long-term rates.
Forward guidance
Promising future rates. The promise itself shapes behaviour today.
Reserve requirements
Minimum banks must hold. Higher = less lending = slower money creation.
Lender of last resort
Emergency lending in a crisis to stop bank runs spreading.
Taylor rule
A formula linking the ideal rate to inflation and the output gap.
Topic 06 · Government

Fiscal Policy

Governments use spending and taxation to manage the economy. Unlike monetary policy, it requires political decisions — and takes time.

In plain terms. When the economy is sick, the government acts like a doctor — spending more and cutting taxes to inject energy. When it's overheating, it does the opposite.
Fiscal policy simulator
Government spending (ΔG)0%
Tax rate change0%
Fiscal multiplier1.5×
Neutral stance. The multiplier (1.5×) shows how much GDP rises per £1 of government spending.
GDP impact vs deficit
GDP impactDeficit effect
Key fiscal concepts
Expansionary
More spending + tax cuts. For recessions. Raises the deficit. Keynes' prescription.
Contractionary
Less spending + tax rises. Cuts the deficit but can deepen a downturn.
Automatic stabilisers
Benefits rise and taxes fall automatically in slumps — no legislation needed.
Crowding out
Heavy borrowing raises rates, reducing private investment.
Ricardian equivalence
People save stimulus expecting future tax rises — blunting its effect.
Debt sustainability
Sustainable if growth exceeds the interest rate (g > r).
Topic 07 · Credit

Money & Banking

Most money doesn't come from central banks — it's created by commercial banks every time they make a loan.

In plain terms. When a bank lends you £1,000, it doesn't take it from another account. It creates new money — typing numbers into a computer. That's how 97% of money is made.
The money multiplier

Reserve ratio (%)10%
With 10% reserves, £1,000 creates up to £10,000 in broad money.

From narrow to broad money
M0 base
Physical cash + bank reserves. Directly controlled by the central bank.
M1
M0 + current accounts. The most liquid money you spend daily.
M2
M1 + savings accounts. Broader — useful for predicting inflation.
Velocity
How often each £ changes hands. MV = PQ. If V falls, more money may not raise prices.
Lender of last resort
Central bank rescues solvent banks in a crisis. Bagehot's rule, 1873.
Seigniorage
Government profit from issuing currency — effectively an inflation tax.
Topic 08 · Open Economy

International Trade

Ricardo's comparative advantage showed trade benefits everyone — even if one country is better at making everything.

In plain terms. Even if you're better at cooking AND cleaning than your flatmate, it still pays to specialise — you cook, they clean. Both end up better off.
Trade flow simulator

Trade roughly balanced. A weaker currency makes exports cheaper and imports dearer — improving the balance over time.

Key trade concepts
Comparative advantage
Specialise in what you're relatively best at. Trade benefits both sides.
Balance of payments
Current account + capital account. Must sum to zero by definition.
Exchange regimes
Fixed, floating or managed. Each trades off monetary independence.
Purchasing power parity
Adjusts for price differences to compare incomes fairly.
J-curve
After devaluation, the trade balance worsens before it improves.
Mundell-Fleming
You can't have open capital, a fixed rate and independent monetary policy at once.
Topic 09 · The Long Run

Long-run Growth

The Solow model shows capital alone can't sustain growth. Only technology raises living standards over the long run.

In plain terms. Hiring more farm workers helps — but the 100th worker barely adds anything. That's diminishing returns. Only better technology keeps raising output indefinitely.
Solow model simulator
Savings rate (s)25%
Population growth (n)1.0%
Depreciation (δ)5%
Technology growth (A)1.5%
Only technology (A) drives long-run per-capita growth. Capital brings you to a steady state — technology keeps raising the ceiling.
Convergence to steady state
Capital/workerGDP/worker
Growth theories
Solow model
Growth = capital + labour + technology. Only TFP sustains it long-run.
TFP
The "Solow residual" — growth from innovation, not just more inputs.
Diminishing returns
Each extra unit of capital adds less. Why poor countries can catch up.
Convergence
Poor countries should grow faster and close the gap over time.
Endogenous growth
Romer: ideas have increasing returns. R&D can sustain growth.
Institutions
Property rights and rule of law explain most income differences.
Topic 10 · The Debates

Schools of Thought

Economists disagree — a lot. These are the major schools that have shaped how governments and central banks run economies.

Keynesian
J.M. Keynes · 1936
Government must intervene in recessions. Prices are sticky. Markets don't self-correct fast enough.
"In the long run we are all dead."
Monetarism
Milton Friedman · 1950s–70s
Inflation is always monetary. Stable money growth beats discretion. The natural rate of unemployment.
"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."
New Classical
Lucas, Sargent · 1970s
Rational expectations. Markets clear instantly. Anticipated policy is ineffective.
"You can't fool all the people all the time."
New Keynesian
Mankiw, Woodford · 1980s+
Rational expectations plus sticky prices. DSGE models. Today's central-bank consensus.
The mainstream framework in use today.
Supply-side
Laffer · Reagan/Thatcher era
Cut taxes and deregulate to grow supply. Benefits trickle down. The Laffer curve.
"A rising tide lifts all boats."
Austrian
Hayek, von Mises
Markets know best. Low rates cause malinvestment. Let recessions clear naturally.
"The curious task of economics is to demonstrate how little men know."
Modern Monetary Theory
Kelton, Mosler
Currency issuers can't go bankrupt in their own currency. Inflation, not debt, is the constraint.
"The deficit is the private sector's surplus."
Post-Keynesian
Minsky, Kalecki
Financial instability is built in. Demand-led growth. Debt and banks matter fundamentally.
"Stability is destabilising." — Hyman Minsky
The big debate. When recession hits: Keynesians say spend; Monetarists say stabilise money; New Classicals say nothing anticipated will work; Austrians say let it clear; MMT says spend until inflation appears. All have real-world evidence on their side.
