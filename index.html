📊 GDP & Output
Gross Domestic Product
Topic 1 of 10
GDP & Economic Output
GDP is the total value of everything a country produces. It's the single most important number in macroeconomics.
Simple version: If you added up the price of every haircut, every car, every Netflix subscription and every government service in a country for a year — that's GDP.

Consumer spending (C) is the largest part of GDP in most economies — typically 55–70%. When consumers lose confidence, the whole economy feels it.
CIGNX
GDP breakdown by component.
How GDP is measured — 3 approaches
Expenditure approach
Adds all spending: C + I + G + NX. Most commonly used. What people paid for everything.
Income approach
Sums all incomes: wages, profits, rents, interest. Should equal the expenditure figure.
Production approach
Value added at each stage of production. Avoids double-counting intermediate goods like steel.
Real vs Nominal GDP
Real strips out inflation. If GDP rose 5% but prices rose 3%, real growth was only 2%.
GDP per capita
GDP ÷ population. Shows average living standard — a better comparison between countries.
PPP adjustment
Adjusts for price differences between countries. £1 buys more in India than Switzerland.
🔄 Business Cycle
Expansion · Peak · Contraction · Trough
Topic 2 of 10
The Business Cycle
Economies don't grow in a straight line. They expand, peak, contract and trough — over and over throughout history.
Simple version: Think of the economy like breathing — it expands (inhale) and contracts (exhale). The cycle keeps repeating, and no expansion lasts forever.
The cycle wave — 60 years of output
Business cycle showing output over time.
Expansion — the good times
GDP rises, unemployment falls, businesses invest, consumers spend confidently. The economy is growing. Think of it like spring and summer — things are blooming and everyone has energy.
🌱 Analogy: An expansion is like a road trip at 70mph on a clear motorway — music up, fuel full, and the whole team confident you'll reach your destination.
📉 Unemployment falls
Businesses need more workers. Job vacancies rise. The unemployed find work within weeks.
💸 Consumer spending rises
People with jobs and confidence spend freely — on homes, cars, holidays and restaurants.
🏗️ Business investment booms
Companies build new factories, buy equipment, launch products. Future output capacity grows.
📈 Asset prices rise
Stocks, property and commodities climb as optimism grows. Wealth effects encourage more spending.
💰 Wages increase
Competition for workers pushes wages up — especially late in the expansion near full employment.
🏦 Credit expands
Banks lend more freely. Low interest rates make borrowing cheap. Leverage builds across the economy.
Peak — maximum output
The economy is running at full capacity. Unemployment is near its natural rate (NAIRU). Inflation starts picking up as everyone is spending and wages are rising fast.
⛰️ Analogy: You've reached the top of the hill. The view is great — but the only way forward is downhill.
Warning signs at the peak: Inflation above 3%, unemployment below NAIRU, stock market valuations at historical highs, banks lending aggressively, asset prices rising faster than incomes.
🔥 Inflation rises
With everyone employed and spending, demand outstrips supply. Prices creep up.
📊 GDP growth slows
You can't keep growing indefinitely. Productivity gains slow and capacity limits are hit.
🏦 Central bank acts
The central bank raises interest rates to cool inflation — the classic peak signal.
Contraction (Recession) — the hard times
GDP falls. Two consecutive quarters of negative growth = official recession. Businesses cut investment, lay off workers, and consumer confidence collapses. The cycle's toughest phase.
❄️ Analogy: Winter has arrived. Everything slows down — people stay home, spend less, and wait for spring.
📉 GDP falls
Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth = official recession. Output shrinks.
👥 Unemployment rises
Firms lay off workers to cut costs. Those workers spend less — worsening the recession.
😨 Confidence collapses
Uncertainty causes businesses to delay investment. Consumers save rather than spend.
💳 Credit tightens
Banks become cautious. Lending dries up, choking off spending and investment further.
Trough — rock bottom and the turning point
The lowest point of the cycle. Output, employment and confidence are at their worst. But this is also where recovery begins — the dark before the dawn. Policy stimulus kicks in.
🌅 Analogy: You've been sick for weeks and feel terrible — but that's actually the turning point. Things can only get better from here.
At the trough: Central banks cut interest rates. Governments increase spending. Pent-up demand starts to build. The cycle turns and expansion begins again.

Easy credit, housing boom in US/UK/Spain/Ireland, China driving commodity demand. Classic Minsky moment — "stability is destabilising." Ended in the worst financial crisis since 1929.

2009–2020: The Long Recovery
Longest ever US expansion but also slowest. Fed held rates near zero for 7 years, deployed $4 trillion QE. Unemployment fell 10% → 3.5% over 11 years. Ended abruptly by COVID-19 in Feb 2020.

2020: The COVID Crash
Shortest but sharpest recession on record. GDP fell 10% in one quarter. Unemployment hit 14.7% in the US. Extraordinary fiscal and monetary response — $10+ trillion globally. Recovery was surprisingly fast.
📡 Leading indicators (predict the future)
Stock market prices
Markets price in future expectations — a sustained rally often signals expansion ahead.
New building permits
Construction takes time — permits issued now mean jobs and output months from now.
Manufacturing PMI
Purchasing Managers Index above 50 = expansion. Below 50 = contraction expected.
Yield curve shape
An inverted yield curve (short rates > long rates) has preceded every US recession since 1969.
🕰️ Lagging indicators (confirm what happened)
Unemployment rate
Rises after the recession has already started — firms wait before laying off workers.
CPI inflation
Reflects price pressures that built up over months. Slow to respond to policy changes.
GDP growth figures
Published weeks after the quarter ends — you find out you were in recession after the fact.
Bank loan volumes
Lending rises in expansion, falls in recession — confirms the direction after the move.
🔥 Inflation
CPI · PCE · Demand-pull · Cost-push
Topic 3 of 10
Inflation & Prices
Inflation is the rate at which prices rise across the economy. A little is healthy. Too much is devastating.
Simple version: If your weekly shop cost £100 last year and costs £102 this year, that's 2% inflation. Central banks target 2% as the sweet spot — enough momentum without eroding purchasing power.

Inflation near 2% — healthy zone. The 2% target gives enough room to cut rates in a recession without risking deflation.

Inflation over 8 periods.
Types of inflation — understanding the causes
🛒 Demand-pull
Too much money chasing too few goods. Economy overheating — everyone is spending freely. Classic late-expansion inflation.
⛽ Cost-push
Supply shocks raise production costs (oil, wages, materials). Firms pass costs to consumers. Harder for central banks to fix.
🔄 Built-in / wage-price spiral
Workers expect inflation, demand higher wages. Firms raise prices to cover wages. Self-fulfilling cycle — hard to break.
🖨️ Monetary inflation
Printing too much money increases M without increasing Q. Prices must rise (MV = PQ). Zimbabwe and Weimar Germany.
❄️ Deflation
Falling prices. Sounds good but causes consumers to delay spending. Can trap economies like Japan's Lost Decade.
💥 Hyperinflation
Extreme inflation (>50%/month). Trust in currency collapses. Zimbabwe 2008: 89 sextillion percent annual inflation.
Real-world inflation events — click to study
🛢️ 1973 Oil Shock — cost-push inflation hit 14% in US
🇩🇪 1923 Weimar — bread cost 200 billion marks
🇯🇵 1990s Japan — deflation trap for 20 years
🦠 2021–23 — post-COVID demand surge + supply chains
🇿🇼 Zimbabwe 2008 — 89 sextillion % hyperinflation
🏦 1981 Volcker — raised rates to 20% to kill inflation
👥 Unemployment
Phillips Curve · NAIRU · Types
Topic 4 of 10
Unemployment & Labour Markets
Not all unemployment is the same. Understanding the different types is key to knowing what policy can actually fix.
Simple version: Some unemployment is actually normal and healthy (people between jobs). The dangerous kind is cyclical unemployment caused by recessions — that's what governments and central banks try to fix.

Near NAIRU — the natural rate. Some unemployment always exists from people switching jobs or retraining. This can't be eliminated by policy.
Phillips curveCurrent positionNAIRU
Phillips curve.
Types of unemployment — very different causes and cures
🔍 Frictional
Workers between jobs — searching for better opportunities. Normal and healthy. Part of the natural rate. Can last weeks.
⚙️ Structural
Skills mismatch — jobs exist but workers can't do them. Caused by automation, industry shifts. Needs retraining, not stimulus.
📉 Cyclical
Caused by recessions — falls in demand reduce hiring. The main target of fiscal and monetary policy stimulus.
🌊 Seasonal
Regular patterns tied to seasons — tourism, agriculture, construction. Predictable and not caused by economic weakness.
📊 NAIRU
Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate — the floor below which unemployment can't fall without inflation accelerating (~4–5%).
👀 Participation rate
% of working-age people in the workforce. Falls in recessions as discouraged workers give up looking — masking true joblessness.
The long-run Phillips curve: In the short run, lower unemployment = higher inflation. But in the long run, this tradeoff disappears. Friedman and Phelps (1968) showed the curve is vertical at NAIRU — you cannot permanently trade inflation for jobs.
🏦 Monetary Policy
Interest rates · QE · Forward guidance
Topic 5 of 10
Monetary Policy
Central banks — the Fed, Bank of England, ECB — use interest rates and money supply to stabilise prices and support growth.
Simple version: When inflation is too high, the central bank raises rates — making borrowing expensive so people spend less. When the economy is weak, it cuts rates — making borrowing cheap to encourage spending and investment.

Monetary policy tools explained simply
📊 Interest rates
The primary tool. Raise = cool economy, fight inflation. Cut = stimulate growth. Works through credit channel with 12–18 month lag.
🖨️ Quantitative easing
Central bank buys government bonds, injecting cash into the system. Used when rates are already at zero. Pushes down long-term rates.
📢 Forward guidance
"We plan to keep rates low until 2026." This promise itself is stimulative — businesses plan accordingly without rates changing yet.
🏦 Reserve requirements
Minimum % banks must hold in reserve. Higher = banks lend less = slower money creation. Less used today than rates.
🔄 Open market operations
Daily buying and selling of short-term bonds to keep the overnight rate at the target. How rates are actually implemented.
🛡️ Lender of last resort
In a crisis, the central bank lends freely to solvent banks. Prevents bank runs from becoming full financial collapse.
📐 Taylor rule
Formula: ideal rate = 2% + 1.5×(inflation − 2%) + 0.5×(output gap). A guide, not a law — but useful for evaluating policy.
🏛️ Fiscal Policy
Spending · Taxation · Multiplier
Topic 6 of 10
Fiscal Policy
Governments use spending and taxation to manage the economy. Unlike monetary policy, fiscal policy requires political decisions — and takes time to implement.
Simple version: If the economy is sick (recession), the government acts like a doctor — spending more money and cutting taxes to inject energy. If it's overheating, it does the opposite.

Neutral stance — no stimulus or contraction. The multiplier (1.5×) shows how much GDP rises per £1 of government spending.

Fiscal impact on GDP and deficit.
Key fiscal concepts
📈 Expansionary fiscal
More spending + tax cuts. Used in recessions. Raises deficit. Keynes' prescription: "spend now, worry about debt later."
📉 Contractionary fiscal
Cut spending + raise taxes. Reduces deficit but can deepen recession. Used to fight inflation or restore market confidence.
⚡ Automatic stabilisers
Benefits rise, taxes fall automatically in recessions — cushioning the blow without any new legislation.
🏗️ Crowding out
Heavy government borrowing raises rates, reducing private investment. Partially offsets the stimulus effect.
🧮 Ricardian equivalence
Barro: rational people save stimulus money expecting future tax rises. If true, fiscal policy is largely ineffective.
📊 Debt sustainability
Sustainable if g > r (growth rate > interest rate). Japan: 250% debt/GDP but still functioning because r ≈ 0.
💰 Money & Banking
M0 · M1 · M2 · Money multiplier
Topic 7 of 10
Money, Banking & Credit
Most money doesn't come from central banks — it's created by commercial banks every time they make a loan.
Simple version: When a bank lends you £1,000, it doesn't take that from another customer's account. It creates new money — literally typing numbers into a computer. This is how 97% of money is created.
Money multiplier — how one deposit becomes many
Initial deposit (£)£1,000
Reserve ratio (%)10%
With 10% reserves, £1,000 creates up to £10,000 in broad money. The multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratio.
Multiplier = 1 ÷ Reserve ratio = 1 ÷ 0.10 = 10×
Total money supplyInitial deposit
Money creation through lending rounds.
Types of money — from narrow to broad
💵 M0 (monetary base)
Physical cash + bank reserves at the central bank. Directly controlled by the central bank. The foundation.
💳 M1
M0 + demand deposits (current accounts). Most liquid. What you actually spend day to day.
💰 M2
M1 + savings accounts, money market funds. Broader. Useful for predicting medium-term inflation.
🔄 Velocity of money
How many times each £ changes hands per year. MV = PQ. If V falls, more money may not raise prices.
🏛️ Lender of last resort
Central bank provides emergency funds to prevent bank runs spreading. "Lend freely at penalty rates" — Bagehot 1873.
💸 Seigniorage
Government profit from issuing currency. Printing money is effectively a tax on holders of cash (inflation tax).
🌍 International Trade
Ricardo · PPP · Exchange rates
Topic 8 of 10
International Trade & Exchange Rates
Ricardo's comparative advantage showed that trade benefits everyone — even if one country is better at making everything.
Simple version: Even if you're better at cooking AND cleaning than your flatmate, it's still worth specialising — you cook, they clean. Both of you end up better off. That's comparative advantage.

Exports, imports and trade balance.
Key trade concepts
⚖️ Comparative advantage
Specialise in what you're relatively best at. Even if Portugal makes everything better than England, trade still benefits both.
📊 Balance of payments
Current account (trade) + Capital account (investment flows). Must sum to zero — every deficit is financed somewhere.
💱 Exchange rate regimes
Fixed (pegged), floating (market-determined), or managed float. Each has different tradeoffs for monetary independence.
🔍 Purchasing power parity
Adjusting for price differences so you can fairly compare incomes across countries. A Big Mac costs different in NY vs Mumbai.
📈 J-curve effect
After currency weakens, trade balance worsens before improving — import prices rise immediately, export volumes take time to adjust.
🌐 Mundell-Fleming
Fixed rates: monetary policy is powerless. Floating rates: fiscal policy is weakened. Can't have open capital + fixed rate + independent monetary policy simultaneously.
🛡️ Tariffs & quotas
Tariffs tax imports; quotas limit volumes. Protect domestic producers but raise prices for consumers and invite retaliation.
📈 Long-run Growth
Solow model · TFP · Convergence
Topic 9 of 10
Long-run Economic Growth
The Solow model shows that capital accumulation alone can't sustain growth. Only technology (TFP) drives long-run increases in living standards.
Simple version: Imagine hiring more workers for a farm. The first 10 make a huge difference. The next 10 help a bit. By the 100th worker, you're running out of space. That's diminishing returns — why you can't just keep adding capital and expect growth to continue forever.

Only technology (A) drives long-run per-capita growth. Capital accumulation brings you to a steady state — but technology keeps raising that ceiling.

Solow growth model: capital and output per worker over time.
Growth theories explained
🔩 Solow model
Growth = capital + labour + technology (TFP). Only TFP sustains long-run growth. Capital hits diminishing returns.
🧠 TFP (Solow residual)
The "magic ingredient" — growth not explained by inputs. Innovation, better institutions, new ideas.
📉 Diminishing returns
Each extra unit of capital adds less and less output. The reason poor countries can grow faster than rich ones.
🏃 Convergence
Poor countries should grow faster (less diminishing returns) and "catch up" to rich countries over time.
💡 Endogenous growth
Romer: ideas have increasing returns — one idea can be used by everyone simultaneously. R&D can sustain growth indefinitely.
🏛️ Institutions matter
Acemoglu & Robinson: property rights, rule of law, inclusive political systems explain most income differences between countries.
🎓 Schools of Thought
Keynes · Friedman · Lucas · Hayek
Topic 10 of 10
Schools of Economic Thought
Economists disagree — a lot. These are the major schools of thought that have shaped how governments and central banks manage economies.
The major schools — click any to explore deeper
Keynesian Economics
John Maynard Keynes, 1936
Government must intervene in recessions. Prices are sticky. Markets don't self-correct quickly. Active fiscal policy essential.
"In the long run we are all dead."
Monetarism
Milton Friedman, 1950s–70s
Inflation is always a monetary phenomenon. Rules over discretion. Stable money supply growth. Natural rate of unemployment.
"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."
New Classical
Lucas, Sargent, Barro — 1970s
Rational expectations. Markets clear instantly. Anticipated policy is ineffective — people adjust behaviour pre-emptively.
"You can't fool all the people all the time."
New Keynesian
Mankiw, Romer, Woodford — 1980s+
Rational expectations + sticky prices. DSGE models. The mainstream central bank framework today. Policy still works.
Today's central banking consensus.
Supply-side
Laffer, Mundell — Reagan/Thatcher era
Cut taxes and deregulate to grow the supply side. Benefits "trickle down." Laffer curve: lower rates can raise revenue.
"A rising tide lifts all boats."
Austrian Economics
Hayek, von Mises — early 20th century
Markets know best. Prices carry vital information. Low rates cause malinvestment. Let recessions clear naturally.
"The curious task of economics is to demonstrate to men how little they really know."
Modern Monetary Theory
Kelton, Mosler, Wray — contemporary
Currency-issuing governments can't go bankrupt in their own currency. The real constraint is inflation, not money. Tax drains money.
"The government's deficit is the non-government sector's surplus."
Post-Keynesian
Minsky, Kalecki, Davidson
Financial instability is endogenous. "Stability is destabilising." Demand-led growth. Banks and debt matter fundamentally.
"Stability is destabilising." — Hyman Minsky
The big debate: When a recession hits, Keynesians say "spend more." Monetarists say "stabilise money supply." New Classicals say "nothing will work if anticipated." Austrians say "let it clear." MMT says "spend as much as needed until inflation appears." All have real-world evidence for and against.
